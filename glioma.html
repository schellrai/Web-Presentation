What are Gliomas?

Gliomas are tumors that begin in the brain or spinal cord within glial cells, which support and protect our neurons. The World Health Organization ranks these tumors from Grade I to Grade IV based on their severity. While Grade I tumors grow slowly, Grade IV tumors called glioblastomas are extremely aggressive, growing up to ten times faster than lower-grade types and often resulting in a survival rate of only 9 to 12 months.

The Complexity of Invasion?

These tumors are uniquely difficult to treat because they don't form a simple, contained mass. Instead, they invade healthy tissue by traveling along "white matter tracts," using the brain's own nerve fibers as highways to spread. To fuel this growth, malignant tumors create their own blood vessels and can survive in acidic, low-oxygen environments that would typically kill healthy cells, giving the cancer a distinct survival advantage.

The Multiscale Nature of the Body

Understanding a glioma requires looking at the body on three levels: the microscopic activity inside cells, the way cells interact with each other, and how the tumor spreads across the whole brain. Currently, standard medical imaging like MRIs cannot see individual cell movements or tiny chemical changes. This creates a "data gap" because doctors cannot see the earliest stages of how the tumor is shifting or accelerating using pictures alone.

Personalized Patient Care

To fill this gap, advanced mathematics to link microscopic data with the large-scale patterns seen on scans are being used. By plugging a patient's specific MRI data into these models, clinicians can create 365-day simulations of how a tumor might spread. These mathematical tools allow doctors to predict future behavior, helping them move beyond general guesses to create highly personalized treatment plans for every patient.
